About Me

I am passionate about objectively understanding movement through the lens of data analysis. Too frequently in the 21st century, people can find themselves caught in a vicious cycle of decline beginning with inactivity, leading to injury, which leads to further inactivity. By developing a thorough understanding of the mechanics and control of movement, we can develop strategies to safely integrate movement back into modern life. In the United States, this dearth of movement is resulting in increasingly prevalent diseases of modernity in ever younger swathes of the population, leading to drastic upticks in adverse inactivity-related health outcomes like musculoskeletal injury, cardiac problems, etc.


Humans are biological systems, and are therefore inherently noisy at every level. This makes it challenging to objectively quantify behavior, whether that is the function of individual cells, activity of individual nerves and muscles, or even gross functions of the whole body such as counting steps taken per day. I believe that to significantly improve the quality and quantity of movement in our daily lives, we must first improve the quality and quantity of our analytical technologies, both hardware and software, cutting through the noise with improved tooling and ever larger datasets.

Movement context is also crucial.
